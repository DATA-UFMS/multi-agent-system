##  What is the Customer Journey? 

The customer journey is the series of interactions a customer has with a brand or product as they become aware of a pain point and make a purchase decision. While the buyer‚Äôs journey refers to the general process of arriving at a purchase, the customer journey refers to a buyer's purchasing experience with a specific company or service. 

###  **Customer Journey vs. Buyer Journey**

I've noticed a lot of businesses get confused when it comes to understanding the difference between the customer journey and the buyer journey. 

Here‚Äôs the deal: 

  * **The** **[ buyer's journey ](https://blog.hubspot.com/sales/what-is-the-buyers-journey) ** covers the entire process from the moment someone becomes aware of your product to the point they purchase it (and even beyond). Buyers don‚Äôt just wake up and decide to buy ‚Äî they go through stages of awareness, consideration, and, finally, decision-making. 
  * **The** **[ customer's journey ](https://blog.hubspot.com/service/customer-journey-map-hubspot) ** **,** on the other hand, is all about how your brand fits into that process. It‚Äôs the specific [ customer touchpoints ](https://blog.hubspot.com/service/customer-touchpoints) where you interact with your customers during their buying journey. By mapping out your customer journey, you‚Äôre making sure every interaction is intentional and impactful, rather than just hoping for the best. 



At HubSpot, for example, we break our customer journey into three main stages: pre-purchase, onboarding, and ongoing use/renewal. 

At each stage, we have key touchpoints ‚Äî like educational blog posts or onboarding tools ‚Äî that guide our customers along the way.

Your brand‚Äôs customer journey stages might look different, and that‚Äôs okay. What matters is starting with a clear plan. Let‚Äôs discuss how you can create a customer journey map.

##  What is a customer journey map? 

A customer journey map is a visual representation of the customer's experience with a company. It also provides insight into the needs of potential customers at every stage of this journey and the factors that directly or indirectly motivate or inhibit their progress. 

By mapping your customer journey, you can use the information to improve the customer experience, increase conversions, and boost customer retention. 

Keep in mind: The customer journey map is not to be confused with a UX journey map ‚Äî here‚Äôs the difference: 

###  **What is UX journey mapping?**

A UX journey map represents [ how a customer experiences ](https://blog.hubspot.com/marketing/ux-user-experience) their journey toward a specific goal or completing a particular action. 

For example, I can use the term ‚ÄúUX journey mapping‚Äù interchangeably with the term ‚Äúcustomer journey mapping‚Äù if the goal being tracked is the user‚Äôs journey toward purchasing a product or service. 

However, [ UX journey mapping ](https://blog.hubspot.com/service/customer-experience-vs-user-experience) can also be used to map the journey (i.e., actions taken) towards other goals, such as using a specific product feature. 

###  **Why is customer journey mapping important?**

I‚Äôve learned that the customer journey isn‚Äôt as simple as it looks. It‚Äôs easy to think: offer a product ‚Üí customer buys. But honestly, it‚Äôs way more intricate. 

Before a customer even knows about your product, they need to know they have a problem and know that it needs solving. Sometimes you need additional education to get them to that point. 

Throughout their journey, they‚Äôll hit different touchpoints. Maybe they‚Äôll see a competitor‚Äôs ad, read a review, hop on a sales call, or try out a demo. Each of those interactions shapes how they feel about your brand. 

Here‚Äôs something I find interesting: [ 80% of customers ](http://www.salesforce.com/resources/articles/customer-expectations/) value their experience with a company just as much as the product itself. 

By mapping out the customer journey, I‚Äôve seen how it gives marketing, [ sales ](https://www.hubspot.com/products/sales) , and service teams a clear understanding of each stage. It reduces friction and leads to happier customers. And when we meet customer needs quickly? We see higher brand loyalty. 

That‚Äôs huge ‚Äî so don‚Äôt miss out on the power of customers.

When I‚Äôm mapping the customer journey, I focus on real data, not assumptions. It takes the form of both solicited data ‚Äî when I ask customers for their input ‚Äî and unsolicited data that gives insights into their actual behaviors.

##  The Customer Journey Mapping Process 

Customer journey mapping is the process of creating a customer journey map‚Äîthe visual representation of a company‚Äôs customer experience. It compiles a customer‚Äôs experience as they interact with a business and combines the information into a visual map. 

This process provides insights that help you understand how customers experience their journeys and identify potential bottlenecks. 

**Note that most customer journeys aren‚Äôt linear. Instead, buyers often experience a back-and-forth, cyclical, multi-channel journey with multiple touchpoints.**

###  What is a touchpoint in a customer journey map? 

I want to make sure we‚Äôre on the same page about what a touchpoint really means in the context of a customer journey map. Touchpoints refer to every instance your business comes in direct contact with a potential or existing customer. It could be online, in-person, through your messaging, website, or app and any number of other circumstances. What you need to know is every time your customer interacts with you, they form an opinion of your business.

###  **5\. List out all touchpoints.**

I always start by listing the touchpoints where customers interact with the brand. 

These moments, no matter how small, are when customers form an opinion, whether it‚Äôs great or not-so-great. Think of it this way: If I saw a display ad for your business or ran into a 404 error page, those would be touchpoints that matter. 

It‚Äôs easy to forget that your brand isn‚Äôt just the website. It extends beyond that ‚Äî into social media, email campaigns, paid ads, and even customer service interactions. What I‚Äôve found is that mapping out these touchpoints helps spot areas for improvement in the customer journey. 

Once you've got your list, you‚Äôll start to see patterns. Are customers using fewer touchpoints than expected? That might mean they‚Äôre leaving your site too early. Are they interacting more than expected? Maybe your website requires too many steps to get anything done. 

Either way, it‚Äôs a signal that tells you how smooth or bumpy the journey is. 

When I do this, I don‚Äôt just stop at the website. I take a broad view ‚Äî Google searches, third-party reviews, and mentions on social media. A quick Google search of your brand can show you all the places customers might be finding you. 

Then, I back it up with data from Google Analytics to see where the traffic is actually coming from. From there, I narrow down the most important touchpoints, the ones that are really driving action.

For me, mapping the customer journey isn‚Äôt finished until I‚Äôve walked in the customer‚Äôs shoes. By experiencing the journey firsthand, I‚Äôve found that you can uncover subtle pain points or areas of friction that might not be evident in the data. 

I like to follow the path for each person ‚Äî scrolling through their social media, reading their emails, doing a search ‚Äî to really understand where they might get stuck or drop off. 

For example, I might: 

  * Search for my product or service using. 
  * Search for a competitor‚Äôs product or service. 
  * Sign up for my own email list. 
  * Navigate my website or app with fresh eyes. 
  * Reach out to support. 



Each of these items can provide insights that offer additional context to your data. For example, while analytics might show a high bounce rate on a page, manually going through the customer journey might help you realize the page loads slowly or has unclear navigation. 

###  **8\. Analyze your results.**

It's important to understand the customer journey map is just the start. The real value comes from analyzing the results for actionable insights. 

I‚Äôll ask questions like: 

  * Are people visiting our site but not converting? 
  * Are we meeting their needs at each step? 
  * What are the top-performing touchpoints (and the lowest)? 
  * What friction are people experiencing? 



This process helps me see where we can improve. Analyzing the results will help me pinpoint where customer needs aren‚Äôt fully met and make sure we‚Äôre delivering a valuable experience. 

Keep in mind that you can use the process to test certain assumptions and validate gut feelings. However, it‚Äôs important to keep an open mind because it‚Äôs almost guaranteed you‚Äôll find something unexpected.

These customer journey maps are the most widely used type. They visualize the actions, thoughts, and emotions your customers currently experience while interacting with your company. They‚Äôre best used for continually improving the customer journey.

These maps are best for illustrating your vision and setting clear, strategic goals.

##  Customer Journey Mapping Best Practices 

  1. Set a goal for the journey map. 
  2. Survey customers to understand their buying journey. 
  3. Ask customer service reps about the questions they receive most frequently. 
  4. Consider UX journey mapping for each buyer persona. 
  5. Review and update each journey map after every major product release. 
  6. Make the customer journey map accessible to cross-functional teams. 



###  **1\. Set a goal for the journey map.**

Determine whether you aim to improve the buying experience or launch a new product. Knowing what the journey map needs to tell you can prevent scope creep on a large project like this. 

###  **2\. Survey customers to understand their buying journey.**

I‚Äôve learned that what I _think_ I know about my customers and what they‚Äôre actually going through can be totally different. So, speak to your customers directly to get an accurate snapshot of their journey. 

###  **3\. Ask customer service reps about the questions they receive most frequently.**

Sometimes, customers need to be made aware of their specific pain points, and that‚Äôs where your customer service reps come in. 

They can help fill in the gaps and translate customer pain points into business terms you and your team can understand and act on. 

###  **4\. Consider customer journey mapping for each buyer persona.**

One thing I‚Äôve come to understand is that no two customers are the same. 

Demographics, psychographics, and even how long someone has been a customer can determine how a person interacts with your business and makes purchasing decisions. 

That‚Äôs why I create journey maps tailored to each key persona. 

###  **5\. Review and update each journey map after every major product release.**

The customer‚Äôs buying process changes every time your product or service changes. Even slight tweaks, like adding an extra field to a form, can become a significant roadblock. 

So, review the customer journey map before and after implementing changes. 

###  **6\. Make the customer journey map accessible to cross-functional teams.**

One thing I always do is make the journey map accessible to everyone, not just my team. Having it out there helps other teams give feedback and keeps everyone aligned with the customer‚Äôs experience. 

If you want to get a head start on your map and analysis, try HubSpot‚Äôs tools and [ create a journey report ](https://knowledge.hubspot.com/reports/create-a-journey-report) . 

##  **Customer Journey Map Design**

Now that you know what goes into making a customer journey map, let‚Äôs take a little time to dive into design. How you lay out your journey map ‚Äî from spacing to the colors you use ‚Äî makes all of the difference. 

I highly recommend the following best practices: 

###  **Create a clear layout and hierarchy.**

Your layout should flow logically, with a clear visual hierarchy that makes it easy to follow. Use bold headings to break up the sections and keep things readable. 

Bullet points? Always a good idea. They make your map easier to digest and keep the info clear and to the point. 

###  **Make the most of icons and symbols.**

Let's be real ‚Äî everyone skims. If your map is a wall of text, most of it will be missed. Icons and symbols are a great way to break things up and highlight key actions, touchpoints, and outcomes. Plus, they can add emotion to your map without needing extra words. 

###  **Leverage color coding.**

Color is a powerful design element that can help you group similar ideas. You can assign different hues to the stages of your customer journey or to certain touchpoints. This helps you organize information visually and draw attention to the most important parts of your map. 

###  **Avoid clutter to create balance.**

Too much of anything is just ... too much. Like I said, everyone skims. If your page is packed with icons, text, and colors, it‚Äôll get overwhelming. Use whitespace to keep things balanced and organized. 

###  **Maintain consistency.**

Your customer journey map should be consistent throughout. Pick a font family, color palette, and font sizes. Then, make sure you follow these guidelines throughout your journey map. Bonus points if your elements align with your company branding. 

##  **Benefits of Customer Journey Mapping**

Customer journey mapping is something that every growing business should be doing, whether you‚Äôre a team of one or have hundreds of employees. 

As [ Alexis Trammel, ](https://www.linkedin.com/in/alexistrammell/) Chief Growth Officer at Stratabeat, says, ‚ÄúMarketers who are trying to perfectly document their customers' journeys may be setting themselves up for failure. Especially in B2B when cycles are longer, the stakes are higher, and research is KEY.‚Äù 

In addition to better targeting and serving your audience, you get the following benefits from using customer journey mapping: 

###  **1\. You can refocus your company with an inbound perspective.**

Instead of chasing customers with outbound tactics, let them come to you with inbound marketing. Outbound marketing feels like yelling into the void ‚Äî costly, interruptive, and let‚Äôs face it, annoying. No one likes being interrupted. 

Inbound marketing flips the script. You create content that customers are already searching for, grabbing their attention before you even think about selling. A customer journey map helps you see what‚Äôs catching their eye and what‚Äôs making them turn away. This is where all that data I mentioned earlier really shines. 

Trammel shares, "For [ Stratabeat's ](https://www.linkedin.com/company/stratabeat/) clients, we perform a gap analysis with this content marketing funnel in mind. If we notice something is missing, we add it to our content calendar. And we prioritize content that leans lower down the funnel, knowing AI may be answering many of those TOFU questions for the Unaware or Problem-Aware audiences.‚Äù 

To nail your customer journey map, use real evidence from customer surveys and [ marketing analytics software ](https://www.hubspot.com/products/marketing/analytics) . This will give you a crystal-clear picture of how your audience behaves and what they're interested in. 

###  **2\. You can create a new target customer base.**

Understanding the customer journey means knowing your customers inside out. Broad targeting wastes time and money, so why bother? 

Instead, I recommend deep diving into their needs and pain points. This way, you'll uncover exactly who‚Äôs trying to solve problems with your product or service. With this info, you can fine-tune your marketing to speak directly to that group, making your efforts way more effective. 

###  **3\. You can implement proactive customer service.**

A customer journey map is like a cheat sheet for understanding when customers are happy and when they‚Äôre frustrated. You can then jump in with [ proactive customer service ](https://blog.hubspot.com/service/proactive-customer-service) right when they need it. I think of it as being one step ahead. 

For example, my friend who works in customer support mentioned how they used to anticipate a holiday rush and send out messages about their adjusted hours and alternative support options. 

Customers were prepared, and no one was stuck on hold in surprise. Offering them a [ chatbot ](https://www.hubspot.com/products/crm/chatbot-builder) , knowledge base, or [ live chat ](https://www.hubspot.com/products/crm/live-chat) gives them more control over how they get help, which leads to happier customers overall. 

And to handle all those inquiries? [ Customer service software ](https://www.hubspot.com/products/service) with omnichannel messaging and AI support is your friend here. Trust me ‚Äî it saves so much time. 

###  **4\. You can improve your customer retention rate.**

With a complete view of the customer journey, spotting areas for improvement becomes a whole lot easier. Fewer pain points mean fewer customers jumping ship to a competitor. 

Here‚Äôs the kicker: Even when people love a company or product, [ 59% will walk away after several bad experiences ](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/services/consulting/library/consumer-intelligence-series/future-of-customer-experience.html) , 17% after just one bad experience. 

Customer journey maps help you catch people on the verge of leaving. By tracking common behaviors, you can address issues before they become deal-breakers. Even if you don‚Äôt save everyone, boosting retention [ by just 5% ](https://www.invespcro.com/blog/customer-acquisition-retention/) can increase profits by 25%-95%. Definitely worth it. 

###  **5\. You can create a customer-focused mentality throughout the company.**

As your business scales, keeping every department as customer-focused as your support teams can get tricky. 

Different departments have different goals; some teams care more about leads, others about signups. A shared customer journey map breaks through these silos by laying out every step of the journey, from the first interaction to post-purchase support. It helps everyone, from marketing to sales to service, align with the customer at the heart of their efforts. 

###  6\. You can rely on data instead of speculation. 

In marketing and business, I find that lots of decisions ‚Äî far too many, really ‚Äî are made based on intuition. While, in my experience, there‚Äôs a lot to those gut feelings, which are based on years of experience. 

However, if you‚Äôre wrong, there‚Äôs a lot at stake. In today‚Äôs world with all the data we have at our fingertips, there‚Äôs simply no longer a reason not to involve data in the decision making process and either validate those gut feelings or prove them wrong. 

Email engagement and CRO specialist [ Alice Brown ](https://www.alicewritescopy.com/understanding-obstacles) expands on this further, ‚ÄúBeing able to triangulate your interview insights with survey responses and other sources of data means you‚Äòre no longer relying on speculation or ICPs dreamed up in a boardroom to base your decisions on. Instead, touchpoints can be confidently built on data. This is how you create touchpoints that don‚Äôt just connect emotionally with your customers, but they also drive clicks and conversions.‚Äù
